In the CIS, “integrative studies” refers to learning that intentionally combines diverse methods, experiences, learning styles, subject matters and on- or off-campus resources. The college has a long-standing commitment to integrative studies, evident in its many successful interdisciplinary majors and concentrations, in the 30-year history of the St. Olaf Paracollege and in the faculty’s ability to combine diverse approaches to course subjects. The CIS’s principal activity is to support students pursuing individual, integrative majors, but it also offers a small curriculum of seminars and colloquia exemplifying and encouraging integrative learning and helps students to identify and exploit opportunities for integrative learning on and off campus. In all these activities, the CIS's goal is to enhance the coherence of students’ academic careers by encouraging them to make meaningful connections among the many parts of their educational experience and by helping them build bridges between the college and other communities.

The Individual Major

The CIS certifies student-designed, integrative majors. A student may propose any sequence of courses, seminars, independent studies or experiential learning as the means of pursuing an individual major. Among other things, the student’s proposal must include (1) a description of the proposed subject matter; (2) a list of 10 or more proposed courses and other learning experiences and an explanation of how each contributes to the major; (3) an initial proposal for a two-semester senior integrative project; and (4) a summary of the student's preparation to carry out the proposed major. The proposal must have the support of a faculty academic adviser. The coherence, depth and feasibility of each proposal are evaluated by a faculty review committee convened by the CIS. If the proposal is approved, the student may pursue the individual major with the support and encouragement of the CIS. When the student’s work is complete it is presented to a faculty certification committee for review. Proposals for individual majors may be submitted any time during the sophomore year and at the beginning of the junior year. Illustrative examples of recent approved individual majors include: Social Policy; Health, Illness, and Medicine; Bilingualism; Conceptualizing Cultural Difference; Religion and Society in the U.S.; Speech & Language (Science of Human Communication); Dynamics of Colonialism; Peacemaking and the Arts; Environmental Discourse Analysis.

The Web Portfolio

To help students with individual majors to build meaningful connections among the various parts of their undergraduate careers and to demonstrate the coherence of their major work, each maintains a web portfolio. A web portfolio presents and preserves important formative experiences and academic work. It is maintained in an electronic hyper medium (like the World Wide Web) that allows students to make explicit links among the parts of their work, to the work of other students or to other sites of public discussion. The web portfolio is presented to the faculty certification committee as part of the work of the major.

For specific requirements for the individual major or for information about other activities of the Center for Integrative Studies, please contact the director or program coordinator.

NEW COURSES

234 Human Geography
This course examines the interrelationships between peoples and their environments in different regions of the world and fosters an understanding of how cultures and individuals order their environment. The course explores both rural and urban landscapes. Students will also consider underlying factors which help explain the spatial distributions of settlement types, livelihoods, health and social groups. Offered most years.

252 History and Religion in South Africa (abroad)
This interdisciplinary course explores the multiple and contested histories of South Africa and the modern and ancient forces that have shaped its complex religious landscape; the political and cultural significance of religion; and how people have conceptualized and acted upon places, events, and individuals they hold to be sacred. Students will learn about links between religion and history by visiting historical sites and discovering their meaning through literature and lectures, theater, music, storytelling and conversation. Offered during Interim.

COURSES

INTEGRATIVE SEMINARS

Faculty in the CIS offer integrative seminars on selected topics, open to students in the sophomore year or later. These seminars model the integration of tools and resources from various disciplines in new approaches to their subjects. Seminars in 2004-05:

210 Biology and Theology: Making Meaning from Mystery
A conversation between methods and conclusions of Christian theology and biology. Field and laboratory investigations, along with accounts of scientific discoveries, introduce the nature of scientific inquiry as a way of understanding natural phenomena. Concurrently, we explore traditional Christian understandings of how God is related to the natural world, both normatively and historically. Offered Spring 2005.

211 American Musical Theater
This course explores the development of a uniquely American theatrical form from the 1850's to the present. It cultivates understanding of the form of musical theater, and uses musical theater as a lens through which to view developments in American culture. The course incorporates study of recordings, films, and scores, as well as popular and scholarly research into musical theater. Students will see and analyze performances, but this is not a performance course. Offered Fall 2004.

212 Sport and Religion: An Inquiry into American Cultural Values
Sport and religion are two significant aspects of American culture. One could say sport has become a religion in our culture — with saints, ruling patriarchs (coaches, managers, owners), “gods” (stars and superstars), high councils, scribes (journalists), shrines (halls of fame), houses of worship, symbols of faith (trophies, game balls, clothing, shoes), and both true believers and converts. Sport has been used to promote religion, and it has been used as a means of enhancing faith. But is sport actually religious? This course examines this question and others in an attempt to come to a better understanding of the roles of sport and religion in our culture.

THEMATIC COLLOQUIA

250 Thematic Colloquium (.50)
Each semester the CIS offers a thematic colloquium, open to juniors and seniors. Colloquia allow students to examine a broad, interdisciplinary topic or question using the tools and knowledge of their majors. Each new theme is connected to lectures, performances and other campus events, which provide starting points for discussion. Each semester’s topic is announced shortly before registration. Colloquium topics have included “Education and Social Responsibility,” “Expertise,” “Security,” “Pursuing the New,” “Ways of Knowing,” “Authenticity,” and “Tradition.”

INTEGRATIVE SENIOR PROJECT

Students with individual majors register for their senior integrative projects in each semester of the senior year. The first semester, generally used for research and other preparation in consultation with the student's adviser, receives a P/N evaluation; the second semester, and final presentation of the project to the faculty certification committee, receives a grade. A brief description of the student's major and senior project appear on the official transcript.
