Interdisciplinary Studies

Director, 2006-07: Solveig Zempel (Norwegian), associate dean, Interdisciplinary and General Studies

Interdisciplinary courses use the resources of several disciplines to investigate topics that cut across departmental lines. Often team taught, these courses raise awareness about the distinctive methodologies and conceptual frameworks of different disciplines and their strengths and possible biases in describing, explaining, and evaluating reality.

COURSES

230 Communicating Science and Mathematics (0.25 Credit)

Oral communication is an essential part of science and mathematics. Students work closely with a faculty member to learn and practice important aspects of communicating technical information to both expert and non-expert audiences. Enrollment by permission of instructor only. Prerequisite: Previous participation in summer communication series. P/N only. Offered both semesters.

234 Human Geography of the Middle East

This course provides an examinaton and application of the key content, skills, and perspectives of human geography. The lens of the geographer focuses on the spatial disctribution of phenomena over the surface of the earth, asking the questions “where” and “why there.” The practices and skills of geography are used to investigate a variety of issues in the Middle East, including environmental problems, the culture and management of sacred places, and the reasons for war and the need for peace.

238 War and Peace

An interdisciplinary approach to war and peace, this course incorporates the dual perspective of philosophy and history. Students study various ethical arguments as shaped in different cultures. Examples are drawn from ancient Athens, medieval Christendom, Asia, Islam, and the modern West; ethical topics include: pacifism, utilitarianism, divine command ethics, natural law theory, feminism, and cultural relativism. The course begins with warfare as treated in the Bible and concludes with nuclear terrorism.

239 Peacebuilding and Conflict Management

Peacebuilding and conflict management is a growing field that engages international organizations and governments as well as civil society organizations and community activists. This course provides an introduction to the field and an opportunity to apply this knowledge to current events through case studies and practice in conflict management skills. Topics include cultures of peace, the role of history and future in conflict, conflict analysis and dynamics, intervention strategies, identity, and reconciliation. Offered during Interim.

249 Mare Balticum(Abroad)

This international travel course explores economic and social developments along the eastern shore of the Baltic Sea. Topics of interest range from the Hanseatic League of the 13th century to the current transition from Soviet-style central planning to a Western European social market system. Offered during Interim.

252 History and Religion in South Africa (Abroad)

This interdisciplinary course explores the multiple and contested histories of South Africa and the modern and ancient forces that have shaped its complex religious landscape; the political and cultural significance of religion; and how people have conceptualized and acted upon places, events, and individuals they hold to be sacred. Students learn about links between religion and history by visiting historical sites and discovering their meaning through literature and lectures, theater, music, storytelling, and conversation. Offered during Interim.

255 Hospital Health Care and the Physician (Off-Campus)

Students explore health care at a major metropolitan hospital (Lutheran General Hospital, Park Ridge, Ill.), investigating the variety of services designed to treat patients as whole beings — including pastoral patient care, day care for preschool and elderly, and treatment for substance abuse. Students meet weekly in group seminars in addition to their observations at the hospital and conversations with health care professionals. Prerequisite: apply through instructor before registration. P/N grading only. Offered during Interim.

258 Theater in London (Abroad)

Students study drama and theater through the reading of dramatic criticism and plays, attendance at approximately 20 performances, group discussions, guest lectures, and tours. London, the theatrical center of the English-speaking world, enables students to experience a wide variety of theatrical performances ranging from traditional to modern. Excursions to Stratford-upon-Avon, Oxford, and Canterbury offer additional theater perspectives. Offered during Interim.
